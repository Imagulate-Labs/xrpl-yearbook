# THE COMMONS POOL

> **The Yearbook does not pay people for holding. It funds the community for showing up.**

The Commons Pool is how the XRP Yearbook turns real revenue into real community — meetups, travel, creators, gatherings — without ever becoming a token-yield scheme. It keeps the Yearbook a living institution, not a coin game.

This page is **doctrine.** It is written to make one kind of drift impossible: no one can quietly turn the Commons Pool into "holder rewards" without **superseding this document with a new, recorded decision.**

---

## 1. What it is

A separate, transparently-managed fund that pays for **community activity** — the experiences that make the Yearbook worth being in. Funded by the Yearbook's own real revenue and disclosed contributions, it spends on people *participating*, never on people *holding*.

## 2. What it funds

- Community meetups and gatherings
- Event-house / venue rentals (event houses, lake days, creator houses)
- Travel and access grants (flights, rooms, tickets, gas)
- Creator and artist support, community contests
- Photography / media coverage of the community
- Launch parties and Class events
- Cultural preservation and the next edition
- *(Optional, much later)* emergency / hardship community support

## 3. How it's funded — net surplus, not gross

Real costs are paid first. Only the **net surplus** flows to the pool. Sources: paid pages · sponsors · merch · physical books · creator editions · event badges · donations · approved contributions · royalties (if any).

**Starting split of net surplus** (adjust later by recorded decision):

| Share | Bucket | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **50%** | Operations / next edition | printing, hosting, tools, storage — keep the Yearbook alive |
| **25%** | Commons Pool | events, gatherings, meetups, media |
| **15%** | Access Grants | flights, rooms, tickets — help people attend |
| **10%** | Reserve | emergency / opportunity |

## 4. Grants are awarded by published criteria — never by ownership

Money helps people **show up and contribute**, by rules anyone can read in advance:

- application · demonstrated need · contribution / volunteer role · event role (photographer, artist, organizer) · first-time-attendee support · verified-member raffle · regional meetup support

We never say *"top holders get flights."* We say *"travel grants are awarded by published criteria."*

## 5. Year-one treasury is boring on purpose

The community's money is handled conservatively and openly:

- Held in a **separate, dedicated account / wallet** — never mixed with personal funds.
- **No DeFi yield, no LP farming, no leverage, no speculative trading.**
- Conservative only (bank / Treasury-style). Any interest **belongs to the pool.**
- **Monthly public receipts** — every inflow and outflow visible. Transparency *is* the trust.

## 6. What the Commons Pool is NOT (the line that can't move)

To stay clean and lawful, the Commons Pool — and any tradeable token connected to the Yearbook — **does not and will not** offer:

- ❌ holder yield or "hold and earn"
- ❌ dividends, distributions, or revenue share
- ❌ profit rights, equity, or investment returns
- ❌ buybacks or burns for holder benefit
- ❌ "market support" or price guarantees
- ❌ treasury income paid to token holders
- ❌ governance or income claims purchased with money

**Membership** stays a non-asset memory artifact. **Any tradeable token** stays separate and carries only **soft utility** (merch discount, raffle / event entry, cosmetic flair, non-binding signal, waitlist priority) — never a claim on the pool or on Yearbook income.

Changing any line above requires a **new recorded decision that supersedes this page.** It cannot happen by accident, by a single conversation, or by a "maybe holders should earn again."

> This doc is about **community funding, transparency, and cultural longevity** — not token mechanics. Token-volume questions live elsewhere, on purpose.

## 7. Not legal advice

This is doctrine, not a legal opinion. The language above is chosen carefully, because terms like *holder profit, dividends, distributions, buybacks, burns,* and *market support* are exactly what triggers securities review (the investment-contract / Howey analysis). Before any of this launches:

- a **securities attorney** reviews the token and the pool, and
- a **CPA** reviews the treasury and entity structure.

Likely path: an internal **restricted fund** now (company-managed, transparent), with a member social-club or charitable structure considered only **much later** — each with its own real rules (member contact, no private inurement, income limits, recordkeeping). None of that is assumed today.
