## The one-minute pitch

Right now, across the XRP ecosystem — and increasingly across neighboring blockchain communities — something is happening: people are meeting **in real life**. They're showing their faces. Communities that used to talk past each other are linking up. Everyone wants to be *included* — and nobody is preserving any of it.

In ten years most of the tweets will be gone. The Discords will vanish. The 24-hour Spaces, the blockchain wars, the Vegas meetup, the Bali meetup — all of it lives in screenshots that will rot.

We've solved this problem before. We solved it a **hundred years ago**, and it still sits on millions of desks: the yearbook.

The XRP Yearbook takes the proven format — covers, superlatives, personal pages, signatures, sponsor spots — and puts it on the ledger. Every signature is an on-chain event. Every class is sealed and never rewritten. And the artifact itself — a true *first edition* of one of the foundational chains — becomes more valuable the further history moves from it, **because it was real when it was made.**

> The XRP Ledger records transactions.
> The XRP Yearbook records the people.

## How a Class works

Each year is a **Class** — Class of 2026, Class of 2027 — assembled by the community and then **sealed**: published, archived, never edited.

### 1. Sign the Ledger (the participation loop)
Members create a page. Others sign it — messages, memories, roasts, acknowledgements. Just like the back pages of a paper yearbook, except **every signature is a verifiable on-chain event.** The retention loop is human: *"somebody signed my page."*

### 2. The cover-artist contest (the annual ritual)
Each year the community runs a cover competition. Artists submit; the community votes; the winner's piece becomes **the cover** — a genuinely valuable on-chain asset as the face of a sealed historical edition.
- The winning artist **keeps rights / royalties** — non-extractive, by doctrine.
- Finalists get a gallery page so everyone who entered is recognized, not just the winner.
- This pulls the *artist* community in — a whole group the builders-and-traders crowd would otherwise miss.

### 3. Superlatives & recognition (the proven governance)
The hundred-year yearbook solution to "who decides": a **mix** of methods so nobody is gatekept by one mechanism and everybody can be included —
- photographers document events all year,
- the committee votes,
- the broader community votes within each group,
- some picks by popularity, some editorial.

Class clown, most likely to succeed, "remember when so-and-so was scamming us, look at him now." Voted by peers — the vote *is* the on-chain record.

### 4. Per-community sections under one cover
The ecosystem isn't one community, it's many. Each community gets its **own section** — its own superlatives, voted by its own people — contributing to one shared volume. The shared artifact is *the fact that everyone showed up the same year*, not a merged popularity contest. Separate "schools," one yearbook. This is what keeps cross-community energy as the *content* and not a fight.

### 5. Seal the class
At cycle's end the Class is sealed: an on-chain artifact (provenance) paired with a **limited physical print run** (emotion). The chain proves it's real; the book is the thing that sits on your desk for 35 years.

## The artifact: NFT + physical book

Two halves that cover each other's weakness:
- **The NFT** = provenance. Sealed, timestamped, verifiable, scarce, can't be faked or rewritten.
- **The physical book** = emotion. The object you hold, the thing your kid flips through, the first edition that gains historical value over decades.

Most NFT projects fail because a JPEG carries no emotional weight. Most physical collectibles fail because they can't prove authenticity. We do both, on purpose.

**Physical = limited pre-order run tied to the sealed class** — print to real demand, no open-ended inventory risk. (Yearbooks always worked this way: you ordered in advance, they printed the run.)

## Business model (proven, ported from real yearbooks)

| Source | How it worked in school | How it works here |
|---|---|---|
| **Personal pages** | We paid ~$800 for a page; parents wrote about us | Premium personal page: photo, story, links, a message — priced for the community |
| **Sponsor / dev spots** | Local shops bought ad space | Project/developer directory spots — *who builds for which chain, what they shipped, how to reach them* (useful now, nostalgic later) |
| **Merch** | Letterman jackets, booster gear | Class jackets, merch — community-run |
| **Photo tiers** | Upgrades, custom shots | B&W, custom, premium framing in the book |

**Keep the line between earned and paid.** Recognition (superlatives, event photos, the record) is earned and free/cheap — that's the *soul*. Personal pages are the paid premium — that's what *funds* it. Tilt all the way to paid and you get a book nobody opens twice.

## Community-driven by design

A yearbook is the rare project where **the community does the work because they want to be in it.**
- Photographers volunteer (and earn rewards/yield when their shots are chosen).
- A committee — run in Discord — handles contests, voting, merch, layout.
- Artists compete for the cover for the recognition.

The founder's job here isn't to do it all. It's to **convene and steward** — the connector role. The reward layer aligns contribution with recognition (chosen photos → reward, LP contributors → rewards, committee → status + stake), the same non-extractive model already proven over two years.

**Structure is the one hard requirement.** A volunteer committee with real power (covers, photo picks, superlatives, rewards) needs clear roles, clear voting rules, and trusted gatekeepers from day one — or it's where the drama lives. We already run this playbook; apply it here.

## Built to be handed off

A yearbook outlives the people who make it. Each class graduates; a new committee steps up and takes responsibility for the next one. There's always someone willing to carry it forward — that's been true of yearbooks for a hundred years, and it's structural, not accidental.

This is the rarest property a community project can have. Most crypto projects live and die with their founder. The Yearbook is designed to **survive succession** — the institution continues even as the stewards change. That matters two ways: it answers "what happens when the founder steps back?" (the next class runs it), and it's what gives the artifact its long-term weight (an unbroken run of sealed annual editions is historically valuable precisely *because* the continuity proves it was a real institution, not a one-time drop).

We start it. We don't have to be the ones who finish it — because it's built never to finish.

## The token: membership in a Class

**The token is your membership in a Class.** Holding it isn't buying access to a platform — it's claiming your place in a specific year of the record. The same way being *enrolled* was your membership in your graduating class, permanently. That's the narrative role, and it would be the right design even without a contest requiring it.

Because it's membership, it's a **utility / access token**, never a speculation play — which also protects the soul of the thing (you can't flip your way to being remembered).

**Your membership lets you:**
- claim your page in the Class
- enter and vote in the cover contest
- vote on superlatives in your community's section
- sign a page — the on-chain action; **every signature is a real ledger event**
- hold / contribute LP to earn member experiences: tickets to XRP events, a free yearbook, a spotlight page

**Direction (decide details later — not blocking the concept):**
- One token, two functions (claim + sign) — *not* two tokens. Half the complexity, tighter story.
- Lore-friendly fixed supply, black-holed (e.g. **589,000** — nods to XRPL lore). A fixed, scarce membership reads as a *limited-edition class*, which is exactly what a yearbook is.

> The token is membership. The *artifact's* long-term value (a true first edition) is real and earned — it comes from preserving history correctly, not from token price. Keep the pitch on **memory and belonging**; let value be the byproduct.

## The real foundation: a social model people already know

The secret weapon isn't NFTs, signatures, or even the XRPL. It's that **people already understand the model.** Nobody needs an explanation for a yearbook committee, a cover contest, class superlatives, signing each other's pages, or alumni coming back. We're not teaching new behavior — we're digitizing behavior humans have practiced for over a century.

That's also why this won't fail the way most DAOs fail. A yearbook committee is, functionally, a DAO — contributors, proposals, voting, roles, a treasury (sales, ads, sponsorships), an annual cycle. But most DAOs start with *governance looking for a purpose*, so participation is a chore. The Yearbook starts with **purpose requiring governance** — the purpose is obvious and emotional (preserve this year), and the committee exists only because the book needs to be made. Nobody joins because they love voting. They join because they care about the outcome. Governance is downstream of caring — which is exactly how healthy institutions work, and exactly why this one will hold.
